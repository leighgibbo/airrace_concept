Day 1London, England12 . 11 . 1919

At 9.05 a.m., on November 12, 1919, Captain Ross Smith's Vickers “Vimy” rose into the air and departed the snow-covered Hounslow Airfield en-route to their first designated stop in Lyon, France.

On board were his brother Lieutenant Keith Smith as navigator and Sergeants Wally Shiers and James Bennett as co mechanics.

Prior to take off, their WW1 bi-plane had been painted on the wings and fuselage, with the registered letterings, G.E.A.O.U., which was a recent requirement for all international aircraft. Ross Smith had quipped that these five letters really stood for “God 'Elp All Of Us.”

As it turned out, they didn't need God. The aircraft itself and the giant twin Rolls Royce Eagle Mark VIII engines were to be their saviours, along with their enormous courage and skill.

Flying the aircraft in an open cockpit, Ross Smith struggled with the constant changing weather elements. To stabilise the necessary weight, a decision had been made not to carry any radio equipment, and so with no way of anticipating weather conditions ahead of them and using only a handheld compass to guide them, they were often flying blind.

Day 2Lyons, France12 . 11 . 1919

Constant heavy cloud, rain squalls, snow storms and blinding unbearable heat were to be their constant companions throughout the duration of the journey.

Their thoughts were directed to their concern to reach Darwin, hopefully in one piece, within the 30 days’ time frame to make them eligible for the £10,000 prize.

Goggles and helmets iced up from intense cold and 90 mph winds. Had to remove them and try to see through slitted eyes. Ross wrote “Never felt so cold and miserable.” However, navigation was accurate. Reached Lyons after a six-hour flight.

Had great difficulty communicating with French bureaucrats who thought us insane to be flying in such weather, and were less than helpful in providing fuel.

Day 315 . 11 . 1919

Encountered headwind. Did not reach Pisa until evening. Decided to land in Pisa rather than risk flying on to Rome in the dark.

Italians at Pisa could not have been more helpful and even apologised for the airfield being waterlogged. The rain continued overnight, the Vimy was grounded as the airport was like a lake the following day. Delayed a day.

There was a danger of the Vimy doing a nose-dive if it could not get enough acceleration before take off, so Shiers tied a rope around Bennett's waist while he crawled along the fuselage to weigh done the tail of the plane as it taxied off.

Shiers managed to haul him aboard at the last minute Water sucked up and whirled around by the propellers until they were soaked through and coated with mud. Finally airborne with difficulty, from mud. They were ‘double-lucky’ for rain fell in torrents for the next week making the aerodrome temporarily unusable.

Battled heavy headwinds to Rome. Ground speed only 50 miles an hour
